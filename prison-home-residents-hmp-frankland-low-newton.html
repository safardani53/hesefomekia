    IT'S a grim, overcast day as a woman stands in the doorway of her semi-detached home and takes a drag of her cigarette.

    As she flicks the end of it onto the floor, she asks: "You got any ID?" and nods towards a high fence with barbed wire wrapped around the top.

    "Prisoners get released and knock on my door to ask for money so I have to make sure I'm safe," she says.

    The building she's referring to is HM Prison Low Newton, a closed female and young offenders institution that has been home to some of the most deadly female killers in the land.

    The jail, which opened in 1965, has housed serial killer Rose West, Baby P's murderer mum Tracey Connelly and more recently murderer Joanna Dennehy, who killed three men.

    Denise Christie lives opposite the terrifying complex - which also incorporates the notorious Category A HM Prison Frankland - with her daughter and has a view of the entrance from her garden.

    The 52-year-old cook claims female prisoners often knock on her door after they are released to ask for taxis and spare change.

    She said: "They release prisoners from there and they knock on your door a lot.

    "They do it every few months, mainly to ask if they can lend money or use your phone. Some of them ask for taxis to Middlesbrough.

    "I don't get worried about answering the door, I just have to politely
    decline."

    Denise admits the sirens and ambulances were "daunting" when she moved in 13 years ago, but within six months she got settled.

    Locals quickly warned her about the potential threat to her then-nine-year-old daughter.

    She recalled: "My neighbour said to keep her inside when it was visiting hours because paedophiles visit paedophiles and rapists visit rapists.

    "So she wasn't allowed out of the house until she was 14. It was a shock to hear."

    Denise doesn't worry for her safety even when former convicts knock on her door because she claims killers are released in a different way.

    However, at times, residents are instructed to stay indoors for their safety.

    Denise said: "You see convoys going past when high-profile prisoners arrive. They stop you from leaving your home but once it's cleared, you carry on with normal life.

    "Sometimes when there's a big case, there are journalists filming outside. I turned the TV on once and thought 'that's my house.'"

    The jail shares the same sprawling complex as HMP Frankland, one of the toughest male prisons in the UK.

    The Category A penitentiary lodges notorious criminals such as killer Levi Bellfield and murderer Wayne Couzens and was once home to violent prisoners Charles Bronson and Peter Sutcliffe, who murdered 13 women.

    Frankland opened in 1980 with only four wings with 108 cells in each, but grew in 1998 when a further two wings were opened – it now has eight wings and houses approximately 850 men.

    Clothes pinched off washing line

    Housewife Sharon Wheatley, 50, has always lived her entire life in the area and insists she has "never felt frightened".

    She said: "Some people ask if I've ever seen anyone escape, but I haven't.

    "Even if someone does escape, they don't stay around here. They'll run off into the farmland, especially over at Frankland."

    One surprising misfortune she did experience was some of her belongings vanishing.

    Sharon explained: "Years ago people who visited the young offender institute would pinch stuff off your washing line.

    "It was never the prisoners, just their friends and family. But now, there are no issues. I feel incredibly safe."

    Sharon says it's easy to spot when a prisoner is about to be released because "there are protesters outside".

    She adds: "There's nobody innocent in there, it's reserved for the worst of the worst."

    The mum-of-one claims it's like living anywhere else and her main gripe is "the volume of traffic".

    "It's the number of workers that travel there – you can't get across the road at seven in the morning."

    Struggle to sell home

    Steven Nixon built his four-bedroom home, which is three doors away from HMP Frankland, in 2005.

    But now, the 56-year-old bricklayer is struggling to flog the property, which is priced at £450,000, because of its close proximity to the prison.

    He said: "The home is up for sale but I've had a few buyers lose interest because they're worried about the prison.

    "Some say, 'What about the people who come to visit?' But, you never hear anything, it's really safe."

    Steven lives so close to the secure prison that it is visible from his back garden.

    "You can't see it if you sit at the bottom of the garden next to the shed," he added.

    "It's the best place to live, I would give it a ten out of ten. I bought the land because I thought it was a great location, it's a lovely area.

    "The only reason I'm moving is that I want to downsize but there's
    nothing to be afraid of. It's the safest place to be."

    'People try to scare you away'

    An elderly resident, who didn't want to be named, has spent her whole life in the area and was there before the prisons were built.

    She said: "I remember when it was all fields and farmland, there were only 30 houses. It was very quiet.

    "When they announced they were building the prisons it was a bit scary but we weren't petrified.

    "Some of the older people in the street would try and scare you but it was interesting, it was something different.

    "I used to work there as a cleaner and my husband was an officer in
    Frankland. It was the best job I ever had, I loved it."

    'Traffic is bad but it's very safe'

    Full-time carer Maz Aghdam, 49, who has lived in the street for seven years, says the area is "very safe" and he's "really happy" living there.

    He said: "A lot of people go for walks around here. We never hear anything from the prison.
