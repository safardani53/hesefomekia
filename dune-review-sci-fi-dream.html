    DUNE

    (12A) 155mins  

    ★★★★☆

    SOME films are so visually spectacular they only get the chance to breathe on the big screen.

    Dune is one such film.

    This stunning remake of David Lynch’s cult-classic but critically panned 1984 movie has all the ingredients of a perfect sci-fi film.

    It feels like a mixture of Star Wars, Lord Of The Rings and Game Of Thrones, if they’d all had even bigger budgets.

    The pace, set by director Denis Villeneuve (Blade Runner 2049), starts slowly, in the year 10191, where the world looks drastically different.

    Villeneuve is clearly a master of creating futuristic worlds that make the mind boggle, yet still strangely make sense.

    Desert planet

    Planets are run by noble houses and the plot — adapted from Frank Herbert’s 1965 novel — concentrates on the gifted young Paul Atreides (Timothee Chalamet), who is born into the House Atreides, the ruling family on the “homeworld” of Caladan.

    His father, the wise and fair Duke Leto Atreides (Oscar Isaac) and mother Lady Jessica (Rebecca Ferguson) make the decision to uproot the family to dangerous desert planet Arrakis.

    While being so hot there that humans cannot step outside when the sun is high in the sky, it is also the source of “spice” — the most valuable substance in the universe.

    But the sun is not the only enemy. There are poisonous characters lurking around every corner, desperately wanting to take the family down. The most repugnant is the Baron Vladimir Harkonnen (Stellan Skars-gard) who is out for revenge after being kicked off his planet.

    The ferocious giant sandworms are terrifying, too. Dune has a stellar cast, including Zendaya, Josh Brolin, Javier Bardem, Jason Momoa, Dave Bautista, and Charlotte Rampling — who features in a short but haunting scene that will have you clammy-palmed with terror.

    Chalamet — despite his slight frame for a lead in an action blockbuster — carries himself with enough confidence and assurance that you could readily believe he could take on a giant sandworm.

    Sometimes the pace slows too much, and the bellowing, ominous score by Hans Zimmer makes the whispery dialogue almost impossible to hear. And with only 12 years between the son and mum actors, I spent the first half-hour assuming they were siblings.

    Dune is a sci-fi fan’s dream — and an excellent few hours of entertainment for those who could not care less about spaceships. As this is Part One of the story, there is plenty of room left for much-desired sequels.

    THE FRENCH DISPATCH

    (15) 108mins

    ★★★☆☆

    WES ANDERSON has crafted a love letter to journalism in his latest, kooky, creative and stunningly beautiful film.

    The writer-director uses the structure of articles in a magazine to tell three stories following the death of Arthur Howitzer Jr (Bill Murray), the celebrated editor of a fictional US newspaper.

    One strand follows Benicio del Toro’s eccentric artist, whose paintings of a nude prison officer (Lea Seydoux) intrigue the world.

    Another is of a student revolution organised by a chess master (Timothee Chalamet), reported on by Frances McDormand. There is also the tale of a child’s kidnapping. As ever with Anderson, the ensemble cast is a red-carpet Who’s Who, with Elisabeth Moss, Edward Norton, Saoirse Ronan and Anjelica Huston just a few of the A-listers to crop up.

    Ironically, a film about a superb editor dying seems in desperate need of, well, a superb editor.

    It is exquisitely styled, as you would expect from the man behind The Royal Tenenbaums and The Grand Budapest Hotel. But it lacks their substance.

    DEAR EVAN HANSEN

    (12A) 137mins

    ★★☆☆☆

    IT is the latest stage musical to get the big-screen treatment, but despite their best endeavours it never seems worth the effort.

    Ben Platt reprises the titular role from the Broadway original in 2016, as an anxious teenager with social anxiety, being raised by Julianne Moore’s single mum Heidi.

    When a letter Evan wrote to himself as a therapy exercise ends up in the hands of classmate Connor (Colton Ryan), who subsequently takes his own life, he forges a bond with the grieving parents (Amy Adams and Danny Pino) and their daughter Zoe (Kaitlyn Dever), who he has long admired.

    The songs by Pasek and Paul (La La Land, The Greatest Showman) are great and performed superbly by the younger cast. “Waving Through a Window” and “The Anonymous Ones” capture the anxiety and loneliness that high school social dynamics can intensify, while “You Will Be Found” is an uplifting antidote to that despair, but Stephen Chbosky’s direction is uninspired.

    There is never a moment that 28-year-old Platt doesn’t look like a full-grown adult, which only adds to the awkwardness of a story whose protagonist has lied to a grieving family about being best friends with their dead son.

    Dear, oh dear.
